ⓓ DESCRIPTION — Import Threshold Methodology

Rule: Commodity must total ≥$100M in imports aggregated across ALL importing countries in a corridor (not per country — per CORRIDOR).

Why $100M? Below this threshold, the addressable market is too small to justify the transaction costs of building cross-border trade infrastructure and attracting DFI/private capital.

Unit of analysis: Commodity × Corridor = 78 qualifying pairs. Example: "Rice × Dakar-Lagos" aggregates Nigeria ($1.2B) + CIV ($340M) + Ghana ($240M) + Senegal ($290M) = $2.07B → PASS.

Result: 78 commodity-country pairs across 15 qualifying commodities. These represent the full universe of potentially addressable import substitution opportunities.

ⓓ DESCRIPTION — Basin-Sink Matching Methodology

Rule: For each $100M+ import, is there a producing country IN THE SAME CORRIDOR that currently meets ≥60% of its own domestic demand (Self-Sufficiency Ratio ≥60%)?

Why 60% SSR? A basin must feed itself before it can credibly export. Below 60% SSR = no exportable surplus pathway even with investment.

Key eliminations at this stage:

• Wheat: Eliminated from most corridors — no SSA country has a credible wheat basin (Zimbabwe borderline exception, explored separately)
• Senegal Rice (SSR 29%): FAIL — imports 71% of consumption
• CIV Rice (SSR 42%): FAIL
• Palm oil (most): $2.8B+ structurally unaddressable — locked to Indonesia/Malaysia. Only CIV has partial domestic capacity (91% artisanal)

Result: 78 pairs → 20 basin-sink matches survive. These are the cases where a trade corridor intervention is structurally possible.

ⓓ DESCRIPTION — Existential Filter Framework

6 binary pass/fail tests — any single FAIL kills the case:

CodeConditionTest Question
E1Production pathwayCan you actually grow/produce this at investable scale and cost?
E2Market accessCan the buyer technically receive the product? (SPS, standards, import regs)
E3Consumer acceptanceWill consumers buy THIS product from THIS origin? (taste, preference, quality)
E4Policy kill switchIs there an unmanageable political switch that kills trade? (export bans, FX rationing)
E5Finance availableCan value chain actors get funded at non-destructive rates?
E6Price competitiveCompetitive via 5 levers? (tariff, transport, working capital, agility, geopolitical)

Special rules applied:

• RED PEA = automatic E4 kill. Countries with a RED political economy rating cannot sustain trade at scale.
• F5 (cold chain) → Existential for perishables. No cold chain = no trade in poultry, fish, dairy.
• F6 (SPS) → Existential for regulated export markets.
• TRADE not LOCAL: Cases must have a cross-border corridor angle. LOCAL-only cases killed unless they have a clear future trade component.

Five Corridor Cost Advantage Levers (for E6):
1. Tariff & trade preference (AfCFTA / COMESA / SADC)
2. Transport proximity (800km truck vs 12,000km ocean)
3. Working capital savings (5-7 day vs 45-60 day supply chain)
4. Agility premium ($20-30/MT for demand responsiveness)
5. Geopolitical risk discount (Suez / Red Sea / Panama disruptions)

The Answer

How our analysis addresses the core questions posed by AGRA's RFP for the Africa Food Systems and Trade Initiative.

Question 1

What is the addressable opportunity?

  • Africa imports ~$100B of food annually — the continent's largest non-energy trade deficit
  • Global food & agriculture market exceeds $7 trillion; Africa's share is under 7%
  • After filtering to AFSTI's 4 corridors, the addressable import pool is $41.3B
  • After commodity and self-sufficiency filters, $29.5B in corridor-commodity demand remains
  • The 10 selected investment cases target an estimated $15.8B in switchable demand
▶ Africa can realistically compete to capture a significant share of its own food imports through targeted corridor investments.
Question 2

What value chains are important?

  • 10 priority value chains selected through systematic 5-filter screening
  • Limpopo corridor: SA Maize, SA Processed Meat, SA Poultry, SA Soya (South Africa as supply hub)
  • Upper Rift corridor: TZ Maize, UG Sugar, UG Maize, TZ Sunflower (East Africa intra-regional)
  • Lobito corridor: Zambia Soya (Southern-Central Africa link)
  • Dakar-Lagos corridor: W. Africa Fish (aquaculture + cold chain)
▶ Priority given to value chains where trade corridor unlocks are the binding constraint, not just domestic production gaps.
Question 3

What have we excluded and why?

  • North Africa corridor — $32.4B removed (Egypt, Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia, Libya trade primarily with EU/Russia, not intra-African)
  • Senegal River corridor — Dropped after quick scan; rice SSR too low (29%), limited corridor trade unlock
  • Non-corridor SSA countries — $23.3B removed (Sudan conflict, island nations, countries below threshold)
  • 8 commodity groups — Cocoa, tea, spices, beverages, confectionery, tobacco, cotton, rubber (below $100M corridor threshold or not food-security relevant)
  • Wheat — No SSA country has a credible production basin; structurally dependent on imports from Russia/EU
  • 10 watchlist cases — Scored below threshold but retained for future consideration (TZ Rice, UG Dairy, Zambia Poultry, etc.)
Question 4

What corridors matter?

  • 4 corridors selected from original 6 (North Africa and Senegal River dropped)
  • Limpopo: Zimbabwe, Mozambique, Botswana, Eswatini ← South Africa supply hub. Key infra: N1, Maputo port, Gweru Railway
  • Upper Rift: Kenya, DRC, South Sudan, Ethiopia ← Tanzania/Uganda supply. Key infra: SGR, Port of Mombasa, TANZAM Highway
  • Lobito: DRC, Angola ← Zambia supply. Key infra: Benguela Railway, Lobito port, Lubumbashi-Kasumbalesa Road
  • Dakar-Lagos: Nigeria, Benin, Togo, Ghana ← CIV supply. Key infra: Lagos-Abidjan Highway, Abidjan-Ouaga Railway
  • 8 food production basins mapped across the continent, each assessed for agroecological suitability, yield gaps, and infrastructure connectivity
Question 5

What are the binding constraints?

  • Market coordination failures — Fragmented supply chains, lack of aggregation, inconsistent quality (Maize, Soya)
  • Transport & logistics — High cost-to-market, poor cold chain, port congestion (Fish, Poultry, Processed Meat)
  • Non-tariff barriers — SPS standards, border delays, regulatory fragmentation (all corridors)
  • Processing capacity gaps — Under-investment in crush, milling, and value-addition facilities (Soya, Sunflower, Maize)
  • Policy & FX risk — Ethiopia FX scarcity, Nigeria import bans creating distortions (Sunflower, Rice)
  • Capital access — Lack of structured trade finance, aggregation financing, and blended finance instruments
Question 6

How did we get here?

  • 5-step screening waterfall: $100B → remove North Africa (-$32.4B) → remove non-corridor SSA (-$23.3B) → $100M threshold (78 pairs) → basin-sink matching (20 cases) → existential filters E1-E6 (9 ICs)
  • Corridor opportunity framework: Food Basin + Demand Centre + Trade Unlock + Investment Potential
  • Evaluation rubric: 5 categories (Market Opportunity, Infrastructure, Enabling Environment, Investor Appetite, Strategic Considerations) each scored 1–5
  • 36 stakeholder interviews conducted to validate hypotheses and identify binding constraints
  • Field visits in Zambia, Malawi, Tanzania (ongoing Feb 16–27) to pressure-test investment opportunities
  • 150+ stakeholders on engagement list; ~60 priority contacts reached
